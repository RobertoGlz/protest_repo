% ABSTRACT
% =====================================================================
\begin{abstract}
    \noindent We document that \textit{apex} corruption scandals---corrupt acts implicating top-level politicians---cause immediate and persistent increases in violent protests across Latin America. Using a panel of \hl{176} corruption scandals across \hl{23} countries from \hl{2008 to 2018}, merged with daily protest counts, we establish three facts. The daily count of violent protests increases by about 59\% in the month immediately after a scandal; peaceful protests are largely unaffected. The effect appears within a week of the scandal and remains positive even four months later. The magnitude scales sharply with the political rank of the implicated agent: scandals involving sitting Presidents generate effects an order of magnitude larger than those involving Governors, Supreme-Court Justices, or members of Congress. Our findings identify a high-frequency, high-magnitude channel through which corruption shapes the political environment in democracies.
\end{abstract}

% =====================================================================
% INTRODUCTION (no section header, Science style)
% =====================================================================
\clearpage
Political violence is among the most consequential outcomes a democracy can experience. Civil unrest disrupts economic activity, displaces investment, and---when met with state violence in return---erodes the social contract that legitimizes the regime. An influential economic literature has linked political violence to economic shocks~\citep{miguel2004economic,mcguirk2020economic}, to commodity rents and conflict networks~\citep{koenig2017networks,dube2013commodity}, to the foundations of state capacity~\citep{besley2011logic}, and in recent comparative work to abrupt withdrawals of foreign assistance~\citep{rohner2026aidingpeace}. Related work shows that protests themselves can produce durable political effects~\citep{madestam2013protests,bursztyn2021persistent}. The contribution of \emph{political} shocks---events that change citizens' beliefs about who governs them rather than their material circumstances---has received considerably less attention. We argue that public disclosures of apex corruption are one such shock, and that their behavioral consequences for political violence are large, immediate, and persistent.

Corruption is a long-standing object of empirical study, with known costs for growth and the provision of public goods~\citep{mauro1995corruption,treisman2007what,olken2007monitoring,olken2012corruption,glaeser2006corruption}. Citizens' perceptions of corruption prevalence have risen in the past two decades~\citep{cantoni2024protests}, and apex scandals---those implicating top-level politicians---have drawn growing attention from news outlets, in part because social media has reduced the cost of diffusing information and enables outlets to reach broader audiences~\citep{enikolopov2018SocialMedia, tufekci2024twitter, dellavigna2007fox}. While exposure to apex scandals can produce desirable outcomes such as political accountability or the unwinding of corruption networks~\citep{FerrazFinanAccount, horacio2019infonetwork, horacio2022metaketa}, it can also erode democratic values~\citep{ajzenman2021powerexample, ApexCorruption}, depress political participation~\citep{foa2020global}, and contribute to the cumulative decay of party systems and democratic institutions~\citep{mainwaring2018party, levitsky2010competitive}. Public exposure of malfeasance by sitting officials changes what citizens believe about who governs them and about the rules under which the government operates. The most consequential corruption-related political moments of the last decade---Brazil's Lava Jato\footnote{See \href{https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=boK0Mh2T5nk}{this video} for more information.}, for instance---were mobilization events, not electoral ones.

We document that apex corruption triggers immediate and persistent increases in violent protests across Latin American democracies. Using a hand-curated panel of \hl{176} corruption scandals across \hl{23} countries from \hl{2008 to 2018}, merged with daily protest counts from the Mass Mobilization (MM) project~\citep{clark2016mass}, we establish three facts. First, the daily count of violent protests rises by about 59\% in the month immediately after a scandal, while the count of peaceful protests is largely unaffected. Second, the effect appears within a week of the scandal and remains positive even four months later. Third, the magnitude scales sharply with the political rank of the implicated agent: scandals involving sitting Presidents generate effects substantially larger than those involving Governors, Supreme-Court Justices, members of Congress, lower judiciary, or other lower officials.

\section{Method}\label{sec:methods}
\subsection{Study Design and Measures}

The empirical analysis exploits the as-good-as-random timing of corruption scandal disclosures in Latin American news outlets between \hl{2008 and 2018}. We use a stacked difference-in-differences design that compares the evolution of protest activity around the disclosure date of each scandal to the evolution observed in the same country in the days leading up to it. The analysis dataset is constructed by: (i) scraping the Twitter accounts of Latin American news outlets to identify the calendar date on which a scandal in a given country is first reported\footnote{We keep the date of the first news report to estimate the effect of the initial scoop and to avoid double-counting through follow-up coverage.}; (ii) building a $\pm T$-day event window around each scandal for $T \in \{30, 120\}$; (iii) merging the country-day count of protests from the MM data to create a scandal-window panel; and (iv) appending these panels into the stacked analysis dataset.

The MM dataset covers 1990--2020 and records the date, size, and location of any episode in which 50 or more protesters publicly demonstrate against the State. If a protest lasts $X$ days, we code its occurrence at dates $t, t+1, \dots, t+X-1$. Where the same event is associated with more than one date in the source, we keep the earliest. A protest is classified as ``violent'' when ``the violence could include anything from riotous behavior that destroys property to shooting at the police or military.'' We restrict the sample to \hl{2008--2018}, the period for which our corruption-scandal catalog is uniformly populated.

We estimate the following equation by Ordinary Least Squares using \texttt{reghdfe}~\citep{correia2017reghdfe}\footnote{In the Supplementary Materials we estimate the analogous specification by Poisson quasi-maximum likelihood to account for the count nature of the outcome, and find similar effects. Robustness exercises using modern stacked and heterogeneity-robust difference-in-differences estimators~\citep{cengiz2019effect, sun2021estimating, borusyak2024revisiting, decade2024dcdh} are reported in the Supplementary Materials. ACLED data~\citep{raleigh2023acled} provides an independent cross-validation source from 2018 onward, and yields a comparable sign-and-significance pattern on the post-2018 sub-sample.}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:main}
    Y_{c(s)t} = \beta \; \mathbbm{1}\{t \geq \text{Scandal Date}_{c(s)}\} + \alpha_{d} + \lambda_{m} + \theta_{cy} + \varepsilon_{c(s)t},
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_d$ are day-of-week fixed effects, $\lambda_m$ are month-of-year fixed effects, $\theta_{cy}$ are country-by-year fixed effects, $\text{Scandal Date}_{c(s)}$ is the disclosure date of scandal $s$ in country $c$, $Y_{c(s)t}$ is the outcome of interest, and $\varepsilon_{c(s)t}$ is an idiosyncratic error term. To trace dynamics we replace the post-scandal indicator with a saturated set of bin indicators for time to/since the scandal: 6-day bins at the narrow $\pm 30$-day window and 30-day bins at the wide $\pm 120$-day window, with the bin immediately preceding disclosure as the reference category.

The identifying assumption is that, conditional on the three sets of fixed effects, the within-year date of a scandal's public disclosure is as-good-as-randomly assigned in relation to the country's protest baseline. The country-by-year fixed effects absorb every source of country-year-level confounding (electoral calendars, ongoing institutional reforms, pre-existing political instability); the day-of-week and month-of-year fixed effects absorb weekly and seasonal cycles in protest activity.

\section{Results}

\autoref{tab:main} reports OLS (Panel A) and Poisson QML (Panel B) estimates of the effect of apex corruption on violent and peaceful protests at the $\pm 30$-day and $\pm 120$-day windows. Under OLS, the daily count of violent protests rises by $0.016$ events per country-day in both windows ($P < 0.01$ at $\pm 30$ days; $P < 0.05$ at $\pm 120$ days)---roughly $59\%$ of the pre-scandal-bin mean of $0.027$ violent protests per day at the narrow window. Peaceful protests do not respond reliably under OLS in either window. The Poisson estimates---which handle the heavy mass at zero in the daily protest count---imply substantively large proportional effects: violent protests rise by $54\%$ at the narrow window and $63\%$ at the wide window. Peaceful protests respond in opposite directions on the two horizons: a $25\%$ increase at $\pm 30$ days and a $21\%$ decrease at $\pm 120$ days, both significant at the 10\% level. The opposite-signed peaceful response across windows is consistent with a substitution from peaceful to violent mobilization on the longer horizon, though our specification does not identify substitution directly.

\section{Discussion}

Taken together, our three facts are suggestive of a particular reading of how corruption interacts with the political environment of a democracy. Citizens do not respond uniformly to all corruption: they respond sharply, immediately, and durably when the implicated official is at the apex of the political hierarchy, and they barely respond at all when the implicated official is further down the hierarchy. The response is concentrated on the violent margin and persists on a horizon over which institutional adjudication is slow, while the peaceful-mobilisation margin attenuates---and in our Poisson estimates reverses sign---by the four-month mark. We read this constellation as suggestive of three claims that we develop in the remainder of this section. First, corruption is a tangible behavioural shock to a democracy's near-term political environment, not only an attitudinal one. Second, the political weight of the implicated official---the stakes citizens hold in that official's continuation in office---governs whether disclosure mobilises citizens to act. Third, when citizens lose confidence in institutional channels of accountability, the composition of mobilisation shifts from peaceful to violent forms.

Our findings contribute to three connected literatures. First, the economics-of-conflict literature has documented that political violence responds to \emph{economic} shocks---rainfall and crop prices~\citep{miguel2004economic}, commodity rents and conflict networks~\citep{koenig2017networks, mcguirk2020economic, dube2013commodity}, the state-capacity foundations of political violence~\citep{besley2011logic}, the economics of counterinsurgency~\citep{berman2011hearts}, and abrupt withdrawals of foreign assistance~\citep{rohner2026aidingpeace}. We introduce a \emph{political} shock---public disclosure of apex corruption---and show that it produces a violent-protest response of comparable magnitude to the economic shocks the literature has emphasised. Second, the corruption-and-accountability literature has documented that disclosures of malfeasance change voting behaviour, particularly at the municipal level~\citep{ferraz2008exposing, ferraz2011electoral, FerrazFinanAccount, ferraz2018audits, horacio2019infonetwork}. The slow electoral horizon has tended to obscure that the same disclosures can have very fast, non-electoral consequences~\citep{madestam2013protests, bursztyn2021persistent}. Our high-frequency design uncovers a channel through which apex corruption shapes the political environment of a democracy within days of public disclosure, well before any subsequent election can adjudicate. Third, the protest channel we document is the behavioural counterpart of the attitudinal evidence in our companion paper~\citep{ApexCorruption}, which documents that apex scandals erode citizen support for democratic institutions and depress political participation. Read together, apex corruption operates through a coherent mechanism that affects both citizen beliefs and citizen actions, and contributes to the cumulative erosion of democratic legitimacy that the literature on democratic backsliding has documented~\citep{levitsky2010competitive, mainwaring2018party}.

Our results help reconcile a set of mixed estimates in the existing literature on the political consequences of corruption disclosure. Studies of municipal-level corruption have produced consistently muted estimates of disclosure's electoral consequences, with some finding modest accountability gains in Brazil and Spain~\citep{ferraz2008exposing, FerrazFinanAccount, ferraz2018audits, costasperez2012corruption} and others finding null or even demobilising effects~\citep{chong2015does, anduiza2013turning, pavao2018corruption, horacio2022metaketa}. We propose that the dispersion in these municipal estimates reflects the limited political stakes at that level: when the implicated official is one of many municipal officeholders, citizens have weaker incentives to incur the costs of mobilisation. Our apex partition makes this argument quantitative. Within our own sample, scandals against Congressmen, lower judiciary, and lower officials---the closest analogue in our setting to the municipal officials studied elsewhere---have median per-scandal protest effects statistically indistinguishable from zero. Scandals against sitting Presidents, by contrast, have median effects on violent protest roughly eight times larger than those against Governors or Supreme-Court Justices, and an order of magnitude larger than those against lower-rank officials. This is consistent with evidence from Latin American presidential approval data that scandals against sitting Presidents are the political margin at which corruption disclosures have the largest immediate cost~\citep{carlin2015cushioning}. The same mechanism that produces muted electoral responses at the municipal level produces sharp behavioural responses at the apex.

A theoretical framework that organises this gradient is the salience-of-stakeholder view advanced by~\citet{jha2019valuing}, building on earlier work showing that institutional context shapes political behaviour~\citep{jha2013trade, jha2022markets, acemoglu2012nations, acemoglu2006origins}: citizen political behaviour responds to the political weight of what is being disclosed, not to disclosure per se. Under this view, apex corruption mobilises citizens because it changes the perceived stakes of the political bargain---particularly when the implicated official is the sitting executive whose decisions, judicial appointments, and command over policy implementation determine outcomes for the entire country. A purely informational account, by contrast, would predict that the magnitude of the protest response is independent of which official is implicated, conditional on the size of the alleged malfeasance. Our gradient---a roughly eight-fold ratio between the Presidential and Other-Apex medians for violent protests, and an order-of-magnitude gap to the Non-Apex median---is difficult to reconcile with such a pure-information story; the data favour the stakeholder reading. The contrast between high-stakes presidential scandals (sharp response) and low-stakes lower-official scandals (no response) parallels the contrast between high-stakes local elections and routine governance in studies of political accountability~\citep{martinezbravo2022elites}, and is consistent with the broader idea that the supply of citizen engagement depends on whether institutions deliver outcomes~\citep{acemoglu2024demobreedsupport, acemoglu2019DemoEcoGrowth, DuranteFutbol}. The substitution pattern visible in the wide-window event study---violent protests positive throughout the four-month horizon, peaceful protests positive in the immediate aftermath but turning negative by the fourth month (Poisson estimates significant at 10\%)---is informative on the same point: it is consistent with citizen mobilisation initially making use of all available channels and then narrowing onto disruptive ones as the institutional response to peaceful mobilisation disappoints. Findings on the erosion of civic and democratic values following exposure to corrupt leaders~\citep{ajzenman2021powerexample, ApexCorruption} are complementary: when citizens perceive that the political elite has defected from the social contract, peaceful mobilisation becomes a less credible means of pressing for change. The mirror pattern in violent government response---which we document in supplementary analysis---is consistent with classical findings that institutional capacity shapes how states meet contention~\citep{davenport2007state}.

The empirical exercise has several limitations. First, our identification rests on the timing of \emph{public disclosure} of scandals, not on the underlying acts of corruption. If the timing of disclosures is endogenous to pre-existing political conditions---because prosecutors or media outlets release information strategically in periods of ferment---part of the effect we attribute to scandals could reflect the conditions that surrounded their release. The flat pre-trends in our event studies make the strongest version of this concern unlikely, but our identification is conditional on the timing of disclosure being as-good-as-randomly assigned relative to a country's protest baseline. Second, our protest data are media-based~\citep{clark2016mass} and therefore under-record small or geographically dispersed events. The cross-source validation against ACLED~\citep{raleigh2023acled} on the post-2018 sub-sample preserves the sign and significance pattern of our headline results, but cannot rule out under-recording in earlier years. A substantial share of our scandals---145 of 176 for violent protests---fall in country-windows with no recorded movement on the relevant margin throughout the $\pm 30$-day window, so the per-scandal heterogeneity analysis in \autoref{fig:boxplots} is identified on a selected sub-sample of countries with at least some recorded protest activity. Third, our observational design cannot fully separate the political stakes of an apex scandal from its press salience: apex scandals are disclosed against a backdrop of greater media coverage, and a substantial body of work shows that media coverage independently shifts political behaviour~\citep{dellavigna2007fox}. We cannot hold coverage constant in our data. The randomised field experiment of \citet{ApexCorruption}, which experimentally varies the rank of the implicated official while holding coverage constant, provides indirect evidence that the gradient is not driven by coverage alone. Fourth, the apex partition relies on hand-coding of scandals into categories. Finally, our sample is restricted to Latin America between 2008 and 2018; whether the gradient documented here generalises to other regions and to scandals in older democracies is a question we leave open for future work.
